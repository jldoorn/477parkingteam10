  I forgot to mention this last week, but I worked with Aaron on initial testing of the sonar sensor. The protocol for using the sensor is to set the trigger pin high for 10us and wait for the echo signal. Once the trigger is set, the sensor sends out a series of sonar pulses, which it uses to measure the distance to the objects within its field of view. Once the measurement is complete, it outputs a logic high on the echo pin for a duration related to the measured distance, where the distance in inches is equal to the length of the logic high in microseconds divided by 148. Aaron and I were only able to send trigger signals to the sensor and view the waveforms on the oscilloscope; reading and decoding the echo signal with the microcontroller will be this week's task. In Figures 5.1-5.4, you can see the waveforms captured on the oscilloscope during our communication between the sensor and the microcontroller. The pink wave is the output of the micro, which is connected to the gate of a MOSFET that pulls the trigger pin to ground (this is a little makeshift level shifter I made from 3.3V from the micro to 5V for the sensor). The blue wave is the actual trigger pin, which is 5V when the MOSFET is off and 0V when the MOSFET is on. The green wave is the echo response from the sensor, where the length corresponds to a distance. In Figure 5.2, I zoom in on the switching of the MOSFET and how it affects the trigger signal, which some ringing is apparent. I believe that since the signal is held high for 10 us, these non-ideal switching slopes will be inconsequential to our communication, but it is something to note and look into for further confidence that there will be no issues.

  The first thing I accomplished this week was designing a block diagram for the major power bus needs of our Aggregator Module (AM). The main goal was to come up with a functional block diagram before designing our schematic in KiCad, with an emphasis on the power needs of the module. This block diagram will be easier to modify and conceptualize, which will make the drawing of the actual schematic much more efficitent with less errors the first time. Additionally, this block diagram helped me understand how all the power will be distributed on the different power buses within the PCB. Once I figure out all the power requirements, I can finalize a selection of buck converters and voltage regulators, and their corresponding circuits, to incorporate within our design. These are the last things to figure out before beginning to draw our schematics.


Figure 4.1: Power Block Diagram of Aggregator Module (AM)


  The block diagram for the AM is shown in Figure 4.1. This details the main power buses that will be on our PCB along with each component connected to each bus. Since the AM has more flexbility in placement and a more central location, it will be powered by a wall outlet via a laptop charger used as an AC-DC adapter. This adapter will output 12V and up to 5A, which is plenty of power for the rest of our devices. The 12V bus will be used for powering the large 7-segment displays. The current plan is to use shift registers to store the segment data to be displayed. The microcontroller will decode the digit to display, write a byte to the shift register corresponding to which segments to turn on in order to display the digit, and the outputs of the shift register will turn on MOSFETs connecting the 12V bus to each segment. We haven't figured out exactly which shift register to use, but it will either have an SPI or synchronous serial communication protocol.
  Currently, there is nothing on the 5V bus. When I had originally drawn this diagram, the OLED we were going to use was a 5V device. Therefore, we would need a 5V bus to power the display and a level shifter to communicate with it with the microcontroller. However, the OLED we currently have decided to use runs off 3.3V, and thus will be placed on that bus. As a result, I have begun to look at different buck converter chips to use. The one in our possession currently is a 12V input, 5V 1.5A output. However, without the need for a 5V bus on our AM, we can use a 12V to 3.3V buck converter and remove the 5V to 3.3V linear regulator. For now, we will continue with the 12V to 5V buck and 5V to 3.3V regulator, as we have hardware with which to test and this allows for us to easily add a 5V bus in the future, but I will be looking at other buck converters to eliminate this extra conversion step on our PCB. I took some pictures of buck converters available in the ECE Shop, shown in Figure 4.2. I will be spending this weekend and next week looking at each of these parts and determining any will satisfy our design needs.


Figure 4.2: Buck Converter Component Wheels in ECE Shop


  Lastly for this module, there is the 3.3V bus. This is where most of our electronic devices will be located, mainly the microcontroller and wifi module. In order to determine the power needs of this bus, I ran multiple tests using the benchtop supplies and measurement equipment. First, I wanted to determine the power loss of our linear regulator at different output currents. As a result, I created four resistive networks to create four different output currents when connected to the load side of the 5V to 3.3V linear regulator. The circuits are pictured in Figure 4.3. The desired output currents to test were 200mA, 100mA, 50mA, and 10mA. I felt this would give me a wide enough range of loss values based on the different power draws our 3.3V bus will experience during operation. To achieve these currents at 3.3V, I designed four resistive networks with equivalent resistences equal to 16.5 ohms, 33 ohms, 66 ohms, and 330 ohms. The first was made with two 33 ohm resistors in parallel, which produced a measured equivalent resistance of 16.235 ohms. The input power to this circuit was measured to be 1,005 mW, and the output power was measured to be 665 mW. The second was made with two 68 ohm resistors in parallel, producing a measured equivalent resistance of 34.042 ohms. The input power was measured to be 505 mW, and the output power was measured to be 317 mW. The third was made with a 47 ohm resistor and 22 ohm resistor in series, which produced a measured equivalent resistance of 68.973 ohms. The input power was measured to be 265 mW, and the output power was measured to be 157 mW. The fourth was made with a single 330 ohm resistance, which was measured to be 329.52 ohms. The input power was measured to be 80 mW, and the output power was measured to be 33 mW. The measurement and testing setup is shown in Figure 4.4. Based on these measurements, the efficiencies were 66%, 63%, 59%, and 41%, respectively. These numbers were higher than I expected, especially at higher power. Therefore, I think this linear regulator will be effective enough to use on our PCB.

  After adding the sensor, I started to work on the power supply for our circuits. While both our AM and CDM modules have similar electronic parts, there are some key differences that make the power selection more complicated. The main points are that the AM will need 12 V to power the LEDs in the larger 7-segment displays and the CDM will be supplied by some kind of remote power source. Originally, I had planned on designing one of the DC-DC converter circuits to place on our PCB; however, with our design efforts focused on our other PSSCs, we decided to look at parts and chips to meed our power electronic needs.

  First, I started looking at the AM power needs. The main components involved with this module will be the STM microcontroller, the wifi module, the OLED display, and two large 7-segment displays. The board will need 12V for the 7-segment displays, 5V for the data lines to the 7-segment and the OLED, and 3.3V for each of the other modules. Based on these needs, I plan on implementing 3 main power buses on our PCB for the AM: 12V, 5V, and 3.3V. The 12V bus will exclusively be used to power the 7-segment display LEDs with current limiting resistors. I haven't quite figured it all out yet, but I think using shift registers, controlled by the microcontroller's GPIO pins, to turn on and off MOSFETs connecting the 12V bus to the LEDs will be a good plan. I will need to research the current MOSFETs, which I own, to determine if they are rated for the voltage and currents expected. The 5V bus will be used for external chips and data lines. I will have to look into a level shifter for communicating between a 5V chip and the 3.3V microcontroller. Lastly, the 3.3V bus will be used for our microcontroller and wifi module. To achieve these three buses on our PCB, we have a 12V 5A wall adapter that will plug into the board from an outlet, shown in Figure 3.9, and two separate DC-DC voltage regulator chips. I plan on talking to the ECE Shop to see if they have any that satisfy our needs, but a quick search online revealed several available at Digikey and Mouser.
